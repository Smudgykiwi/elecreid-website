## Who this is for.

Melbourne homeowners planning a dedicated home theatre, architects drawing a media room into a new build, and builders who need the AV decisions locked before the walls close.

The brief usually sounds simple. A room that looks calm, sounds right, works from one remote or app, and does not leave the family staring at a rack of equipment when they just want to watch a film.

A good home theatre installation starts well before the screen arrives. It starts with the room.

## The problem.

Most home theatre problems are built in early. The screen wall is drawn before throw distance is checked. Speaker locations are decided after joinery is complete. The network rack is placed where it fits, not where it should live. Lighting scenes are added at the end, when the dimming and control wiring should have been part of the electrical plan.

The result can still look impressive, but it will feel compromised. Dialogue sits too low. Bass gathers in one seat and disappears in another. The projector needs an ugly conduit. The family needs three remotes. The room works, but it asks too much of the people using it.

For premium residential projects, that is the wrong order. Cinema is not a product package. It is a room, a control system, a lighting plan, a network load, an acoustic problem, and an electrical installation all meeting in one space.

## The solution.

Bring the home theatre installer into the project while the room is still flexible. Elec Reid plans the AV, electrical, lighting, network, control, and handover together, so the finished room feels considered rather than assembled.

That means the proposal covers the whole system. Screen size. Projector or display selection. Speaker layout. Subwoofer locations. Acoustic treatment allowances. Rack location. Cable paths. UniFi network capacity. Lighting scenes. Control surface. Ventilation. Power. Service access.

The technology should disappear into the architecture. The experience should not.

## Start with the room.

The first decision is not the brand of projector. It is the shape, size, and use of the room.

A dedicated cinema can be darker, quieter, and more controlled. A media room has to live with daylight, joinery, children, and daily use. Both can be excellent, but they need different choices.

For a dedicated cinema, we look at screen wall width, seating distance, ceiling height, sight lines, acoustic isolation, and whether the room can support in-wall or behind-screen speakers. For a media room, we look at glare, furniture layout, concealed speakers, display brightness, and whether the system needs to become part of a wider Apple Home, Control4, or Basalte Home setup.

The earlier this happens, the cleaner the outcome.

## Plan the cabling before plaster.

Home theatre cabling is not just HDMI. It is speaker cable, subwoofer runs, data, control cable, projector power, screen trigger, rack feeds, access points, and sometimes fibre or spare conduits for future formats.

We prefer to over-plan the pathways and keep the visible room calm. A spare conduit from rack to display or projector location is cheap before plaster and painful after paint. The same applies to ceiling speaker positions, wall plate locations, and power for motorised screens or masking systems.

This is where an electrical team with AV capability matters. The cabling, switching, load management, and compliance work are not separate from the cinema. They are the cinema.

## Choose equipment around the brief.

Different rooms need different systems.

A reference cinema might use a JVC or Sony projector, an acoustically transparent screen, separate amplification, a Trinnov or Marantz processor, multiple subwoofers, and a carefully modelled speaker layout. A smaller media room might be better served by a high-quality display, concealed architectural speakers, Sonos or WiiM where appropriate, and simple app-based control.

The right answer depends on the room, not the showroom demo.

We are brand-specific when it helps. We also avoid forcing a product into a room because it is impressive on paper. The best system is the one the family uses without thinking.

## Lighting decides how the room feels.

A cinema lighting plan should be part of the electrical design, not an afterthought.

At minimum, we plan scene-based control for entry, viewing, intermission, cleaning, and all-off. In a premium build, those scenes can sit inside KNX, Apple Home, Control4, Basalte Home, or Home Assistant, depending on the wider project. Step lights, wall washes, joinery lighting, and pathway lighting all need to dim properly and stay quiet during use.

The goal is simple. Press one button and the room settles.

## Control should be boring.

Good control is uneventful. One button starts the room. The display wakes. The processor selects the right source. The lighting drops. The volume sits where it should. The family does not need to understand the rack.

For some homes that means Apple Home scenes and AirPlay-friendly audio. For others it means Control4, Basalte Home, or a dedicated cinema remote. We choose the control surface around the household, not around the installer’s preference.

If a guest cannot use the room, the system is not finished.

## Handover matters.

A home theatre should ship with documentation. Rack layout. Cable schedule. Network map. Source list. Login ownership. Scene list. Equipment serial numbers. Warranty notes. Basic operating guide.

This is what protects the client when the room needs servicing, when a display is upgraded, or when the home changes hands. It also protects the architect and builder, because the finished system has a record, not just a pile of hidden decisions.

## Frequently asked questions.

**When should I involve a home theatre installer in Melbourne?**

Before the electrical plan is finalised. The best time is while the architect, builder, and electrician can still adjust the room, rack location, cable paths, and lighting design.

**Can a home theatre be part of Apple Home or Control4?**

Yes. A cinema can sit inside Apple Home, Control4, Basalte Home, Home Assistant, or a dedicated AV control layer. The best choice depends on how the rest of the home is controlled.

**Do I need a dedicated cinema room?**

No. A dedicated room gives the most control over light and sound, but a media room can still perform beautifully when the display, speakers, lighting, network, and control are designed together.

**What is the biggest mistake in home theatre installation?**

Leaving AV until the end. Most compromises come from late cabling, late lighting decisions, poor rack planning, and equipment choices made before the room is understood.
