## Who this is for.

Melbourne architects, builders, and homeowners deciding on a residential lighting control platform at design stage. The decision usually lands on a shortlist of three. KNX. Lutron HomeWorks. Philips Dynalite.

All three are credible platforms with long track records. The honest answer to the question is that the right choice depends on what the home needs to do over the next thirty years. This post explains the framework we use when we make the recommendation.

## The problem.

Lighting control on a premium residential project is one of the few decisions that lives in the wall. Once the cabling is run and the system is programmed, changing platforms means rewiring. The decision needs to hold up not just for the family’s first ten years in the home, but for any future owner, any future integrator, and any future renovation.

The brief we hear most often is the same. *"We want a system that works, that we can use without thinking, and that someone else can take over if we ever sell or move."*

Three forces decide whether a platform meets that brief. Manufacturer roadmap. Local installer ecosystem. Integration with the front-end platforms the family will actually use.

## The solution.

For premium Melbourne residential, we recommend KNX as the wired backbone, with the architect’s choice of keypad surface on top. Here is the reasoning.

## The short version.

Lutron HomeWorks and Philips Dynalite are competent residential lighting platforms. They have been in the market for decades. Plenty of homes run them well. We have walked into Lutron jobs that have aged gracefully, and we have walked into Dynalite jobs that still hold a tune ten years on.

But we are not building a home for ten years. We are building it for thirty.

When we specify a residential lighting platform, the decision has to survive three things. Manufacturer roadmap. Local installer ecosystem. Integration with the platforms the family will actually use. KNX wins on all three. Lutron and Dynalite do not.

### Manufacturer roadmap.

KNX is an open international standard. It is governed by the KNX Association, with more than five hundred member manufacturers. A KNX dimmer made in 2026 will talk to a KNX presence sensor made in 2040, because no single manufacturer can change the protocol unilaterally. The standard is not tied to a corporate roadmap.

Lutron HomeWorks is a proprietary platform. The benefit of proprietary is tight integration. The trade-off is that the future of the platform is tied to one company’s product strategy. For a thirty-year residential decision, we prefer the open standard.

Dynalite was independent until Philips acquired it in 2009, and Signify spun out of Philips in 2016. The product is solid. The thirty-year question is harder to answer for a platform that has changed hands twice in fifteen years.

### Local installer ecosystem.

KNX in Australia has a deep bench of trained programmers, with gear available from multiple distributors. If the home changes hands in fifteen years, the next owner can find a KNX integrator without making three phone calls. Continuity of service matters more than most architects price into the decision.

Lutron HomeWorks has a smaller pool of certified residential installers in Melbourne. The pool is high quality. It is just smaller, which narrows the family’s options if something needs reprogramming five years on.

Dynalite has a wider installer base, but most of it is oriented to commercial work. Residential Dynalite programmers with experience integrating into modern smart home front-ends are rarer.

### Integration with the front-end platform.

The interface a family interacts with every day is on their phone. Apple Home for most of our clients. Sometimes Google Home, sometimes Home Assistant. The lighting platform underneath has to bridge to that interface cleanly.

KNX bridges to Apple Home through certified gateways and the Home Assistant KNX integration. The mapping is direct, the latency is low, and the integration is documented and supported.

Lutron HomeWorks integrates with Apple Home through a HomeKit-certified bridge. Functional, but the round-tripping between platforms is more limited.

Dynalite typically requires a custom integration to reach Apple Home, which is harder to maintain across firmware updates and integrator handovers.

## What we specify.

KNX as the wired backbone. The architect’s choice of keypad surface on the wall: Basalte, Ekinex, Lithoss, Hager Silhouette, or Core Smart Home. DALI fixtures wired into the bus where the spec allows. Where Apple Home cannot reach a system, [Home Assistant](/brands) bridges the gap. [Control4](/brands) or [Basalte Home](/brands) sit on top as the fallback control surface for anyone who prefers a dedicated app.

The infrastructure does not change. The surface does. That is the standard we hold.

## When Lutron or Dynalite is the right call.

If the architect has specified Lutron or Dynalite at design stage and the wider stack is built around it, we install it to the same standard we would install KNX. Both platforms work. Both have homes that have run on them well for years. The recommendation in this post is not a rule, it is a preference for new builds where the decision is genuinely open.
